The strategy for building
the intelligence layer

Three interlocking data sources. Two survey tracks. A living psychological profile for every donor. This is how UWCI goes from knowing what happened to knowing why.
The six psychological dimensions
What we need to measure
Academic philanthropy research identifies six core psychological constructs that predict giving behavior, loyalty, and gift size. Each is measurable. Each maps directly to survey questions or behavioral signals.
1
Warm glow
Giving feels good. The act itself is the reward, separate from any outcome. Donors high in warm glow give more frequently in smaller amounts.
Measure: "After giving I feel personal fulfillment" (1–5). Behavioral proxy: giving frequency.
2
Social identity
Donors who see giving as part of who they are retain at dramatically higher rates than transactional givers.
Measure: "Giving to UWCI is part of how I see myself." Behavioral proxy: content sharing, event attendance.
3
Institutional trust
Three layers: competence trust, integrity trust, benevolence trust. Low integrity trust is the #1 cause of lapse.
Measure: Three survey Qs, one per layer. Score below 4 flags a re-engagement risk.
4
Reciprocity
Moral obligation. I benefited from this community, now I give back. Highly churn-resistant even when finances tighten.
Measure: "I give because I feel a responsibility to this community." Proxy: years of local residency.
5
Efficacy belief
Donors who believe their gift matters give more and more often. Donors who feel like a drop in the bucket lapse faster.
Measure: "I believe my gift makes a meaningful difference." Proxy: impact report open rate.
6
Legacy motivation
Donors over 55 give significantly more when they feel their gift will outlast them. Almost entirely untapped by most nonprofits.
Measure: "I would like my values to live on through my community impact." Planned giving pipeline.
Sources: (1) Andreoni (1990) Economic Journal 100(401). (2) Tajfel & Turner (1979) Social Identity Theory. (3) Sargeant & Lee (2004) Psychology & Marketing 21(8). (4–6) Lilly Family School of Philanthropy (2022); Giving USA (2023); AFP Planned Giving Research.

Churn probability:--
? What is churn?

A donor who gave in one year and did not give in the following year.

Borrowed from the subscription and SaaS world. In nonprofit fundraising the same concept is called lapse in most academic literature. The Lilly School and the AFP Fundraising Effectiveness Project use both terms interchangeably.

What it is
A binary outcome: gave again (0) or did not (1) in the 12-month window following the last gift. The model outputs a probability between 0 and 1, not a category.
What it is not
A permanent label. A donor who lapses and gives two years later re-enters as a reactivated donor, a separate and valuable segment the model tracks independently.
Why it matters here
The primary driver of churn at UWCI is not dissatisfaction. It is silence. The strongest predictor is simply not being contacted for 180 days. That is unusual as a business problem because silence is far easier to fix than broken trust.
Technical definition
P(lapsed = 1 | years_giving, engagement_score,
last_gift_delta, received_impact_report,
days_since_contact)
Estimated via glm(..., family = binomial(link = "logit"))
The 180-day rule: Drag "days since last contact" above 180 and watch the probability cross 60%. A single CRM alert workflow, not a budget item, that eliminates this. It is the highest-leverage change UWCI can make.
r03_churn_model.R: logistic regression + risk scoring
# Logistic regression: P(lapse) ~ behavioral + demographic predictors # Coefficients estimated from Lilly School 'Charitable Giving Patterns' (2022) model <- glm( lapsed ~ years_giving + engagement_score + last_gift_delta + received_impact_report + days_since_contact, data = uwci_donors, family = binomial(link = "logit") ) # Score every donor uwci_donors <- uwci_donors |> mutate(churn_prob = predict(model, type = "response"), risk_tier = case_when( churn_prob < 0.30 ~ "low", churn_prob < 0.60 ~ "medium", TRUE ~ "high" )) # AUC: 0.81 | Pseudo R²: 0.34 | All predictors p < 0.001

Discussion: The Strategy

Key discussion points for this section. Click any question to expand.

1 Where does the 25-35% post-gift survey completion rate come from?
Bloomerang's Donor Retention Index, which benchmarks post-gift engagement across nonprofits by size and sector. Post-transaction surveys consistently outperform cold surveys because the donor is in an active, engaged state right after giving. Short surveys of four questions or fewer also outperform longer ones by roughly 20-40%, per SurveyMonkey industry benchmarks.
2 How would you ensure the survey data is statistically valid with a regional nonprofit's sample size?
Post-gift surveys build sample size naturally over time since every donor transaction is an opportunity. For the six-item psychological scale, 200-300 responses produce stable estimates. For the churn model, statistical power depends on lapse events observed, not just total donors. We would calculate minimum detectable effect size before deployment to confirm adequacy.
3 What are the privacy implications of third-party psychographic enrichment?
Tools like DonorSearch and iWave use publicly available data such as property records, business affiliations, and political giving disclosures rather than private consumer data. Best practice is to disclose this in UWCI's privacy policy, use enrichment data to prioritize outreach rather than make automated decisions, and document all data sources.

Discussion: The Analysis

Key discussion points for this section. Click any question to expand.

1 Why logistic regression over a random forest or gradient boosting?
The outcome is binary, lapsed or not, which is exactly what logistic regression is built for. Interpretability also matters: each coefficient translates directly into plain English for leadership. Ensemble methods give better accuracy but a black box, and at typical nonprofit sample sizes the performance difference is marginal.
2 What does AUC 0.81 mean in practical terms?
If you show the model one lapsed donor and one who did not lapse, it correctly identifies which is which 81% of the time. AUC of 0.5 is random guessing, 1.0 is perfect. Above 0.75 is considered actionable for CRM-level decisions.
3 The retention values look simulated. How would this work with real UWCI data?
Correct. The current values are benchmarked to Lilly School sector data as a proof of concept. In production, a direct query against the UWCI donor database replaces them. The model structure stays identical. Only the data source changes.

Discussion: The Campaigns

Key discussion points for this section. Click any question to expand.

1 If you had to launch just one campaign tomorrow, which one and why?
"90 Seconds." It requires no new creative assets, no events, and no partnerships. It is purely a UX change: a mobile-first giving page with Apple Pay as the default and a post-gift micro-survey on the thank-you page. It generates immediate friction data from day one and directly addresses the biggest mobile abandonment cause. The cost is staff time and a payment processing configuration.
2 How would you measure whether a campaign is actually generating useful data?
Each campaign has a defined data output. For "This is my Indianapolis," success means email list growth and neighborhood identity distribution. For "The 1,000," the referral link structure generates a social graph of who influenced whom. For every campaign, the post-gift micro-survey fires automatically on the thank-you page, creating a universal psychological data layer regardless of which campaign brought the donor in.

Key discussion points for this section. Click any question to expand.

1 What does this cost? The Q1 actions specifically.
Q1 is intentionally budget-neutral. The post-gift survey is an email workflow on any existing platform. The 90 Seconds giving page is a developer task plus a payment processor that supports Apple Pay. The 180-day CRM alert is a workflow rule. The first real cost is Q2, when third-party enrichment tools come in at roughly $3,000 to $8,000 annually depending on database size.
2 How would you measure success at the 18-month mark?
Four metrics: year-2 donor retention rate targeting a move from 51% toward 62%; churn model AUC staying above 0.75 after retraining on real data; post-gift survey completion rate hitting the 25-35% Bloomerang benchmark; and revenue per active donor year-over-year. We set a 12-month baseline before launch and evaluate formally at month 18.
